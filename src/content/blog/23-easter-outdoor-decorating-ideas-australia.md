Easter in Australia is autumn. In Queensland, it's a perfect weekend — warm enough for alfresco, cool enough for a roast, light enough for an afternoon gathering without the shade crisis of the summer months. Easter Sunday lunch outside is one of my favourite entertaining occasions of the year.

[BOM autumn data](http://www.bom.gov.au/climate/averages/) shows Easter falls during one of the most pleasant outdoor seasons in Australia — with temperatures typically 18–26°C and lower humidity than summer, it's the ideal time for outdoor entertaining.

Here are the 12 decorating ideas that make our Easter outdoor space genuinely beautiful.

![Autumn outdoor table setting for Easter with dahlias, terracotta pots, and white linen runner](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1519671482749-fd09be7ccebf?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&q=80)

## Tip 1: Embrace the Autumn Palette — Not the Pastel Spring Easter

The most common Easter decorating mistake in Australian homes: importing the northern hemisphere spring Easter palette (baby blue, yellow, mint green, pale pink) into an April autumn setting. In an Australian garden in April, these colours look out of place. The light is golden and warm, the garden is transitioning, and the mood is more autumn harvest than spring awakening.

The Easter palette that suits our actual season: terracotta, rust, burnt orange, dusty pink, and warm earthy green. These are the colours of the Australian garden in April — the late dahlias, the autumn roses, the warm afternoon light on the Banksia pods. Lean into this.

## Tip 2: Use Fresh Autumn Flowers as the Primary Decoration

Easter falls in the heart of dahlia season in most Australian states, and dahlias — in rust, burnt orange, deep pink, and ivory — are the most beautiful Easter outdoor flower available. A simple bunch of mixed dahlias in a ceramic jug is the Easter table centrepiece that suits our season perfectly.

**What's in season for Easter across Australian states:**
- Queensland: dahlias, chrysanthemums, Protea, late summer roses, ornamental kale
- NSW and Victoria: dahlias, autumn roses, chrysanthemums, Banksia, pansies
- WA: Kangaroo Paw, Banksia, Grevillea

A mixed bunch from a local flower market or Woolworths florist: $15–$25. Worth every cent.

## Tip 3: Create a Natural Easter Wreath in Earthy Materials

The Easter wreath for an Australian outdoor gate or front door: a circular wire or vine base ($3–$5 from a craft store or Bunnings), covered in fresh or dried native foliage (eucalyptus, Banksia pods, dried pampas grass), with a simple ribbon in terracotta or rust tone. Homemade and distinctly Australian.

Alternatively, Kmart and Target have natural-material Easter wreaths in the March—April range ($15–$35). Choose the ones in hessian, jute, or natural fibre rather than the pastel ribbon-and-egg versions.

![Native foliage Easter wreath on white-painted front gate with terracotta ribbon](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1534438327276-14e5300c3a48?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&q=80)

## Tip 4: Set the Easter Table With Hessian and Natural Materials

The Easter outdoor table that looks most authentically Australian: a hessian or natural jute runner down the centre (Kmart or a fabric shop, $8–$15 for 2—3m), fresh autumn flowers in terracotta pots, wooden or terracotta egg decorations interspersed through the flowers, and white pillar candles in hurricane holders.

The hessian runner is the key — it grounds the table in natural Australian material and immediately distinguishes the aesthetic from the imported pastel-and-plastic Easter approach.

## Tip 5: Place Terracotta Egg Holders and Pots Throughout the Space

Terracotta — as small egg holders, as decorative pots, as the containers for the fresh flowers — is the material that most naturally bridges Easter decor and the Australian autumn landscape. A row of small terracotta pots, each with a small bouquet of flowers or a painted egg, along the centre of the outdoor table reads as Easter without looking imported.

**Where to buy:** Bunnings garden centre ($4–$15 for terracotta pots in various sizes), Kmart (terracotta Easter egg holders, $3–$5 each in March—April), and craft stores (unfinished terracotta eggs and mini pots for children to paint, $1–$3 each).

## Tip 6: Plan the Easter Egg Hunt for the Golden Afternoon Hour

The Easter egg hunt in a garden is one of the simplest and most universally delightful outdoor activities in the Australian entertaining repertoire. The key practical decisions: hide the eggs the morning of the gathering (before guests arrive), use quality chocolate eggs that handle autumn temperatures (Cadbury Mini Eggs, Lindt Easter eggs, or similar — not the cheap foil-covered ones that melt rapidly), and designate specific zones for different age groups if there are children of varied ages.

The Easter egg hunt works from 3pm onward, when the afternoon light is golden and the children have been adequately fed and have energy to spend.

## Tip 7: Decorate With Hot Cross Buns as a Table Feature

Hot cross buns as decoration: a wooden board stacked with a dozen freshly baked hot cross buns from Baker's Delight ($8–$12) placed at the centre of the grazing table, surrounded by butter, honey, and small jam jars. The buns are both decoration and food — and they represent the most distinctly Australian Easter food gesture available.

Baker's Delight, Bakers Delight, and Woolworths all sell freshly baked hot cross buns from Good Friday through Easter Monday. The fruit and spice variants are traditional; the chocolate and caramel versions are an Australian Easter evolution that the children invariably prefer.

## Tip 8: Hang Simple Easter Decorations From Eucalyptus Branches

An Australian Easter feature: a large branch of eucalyptus or a bare-branched autumn tree branch in a ceramic vase, hung with simple Easter ornaments — painted wooden eggs on ribbon ($2–$5 each from a craft store or Kmart), small terracotta or ceramic birds, or natural feather bundles. The branch arrangement sits beautifully in the corner of the alfresco and adds height to the Easter decorating without requiring shelf space.

## Tip 9: Set Up an Outdoor Chocolate Station for the Children

A dedicated Easter chocolate station for children: a small table covered in parchment or a white cloth, with a basket of Easter eggs, a chocolate fountain ($25–$45 from Kmart for a simple model) with strawberries and marshmallows for dipping, and a decorating station where children can ice biscuits with Easter designs. The children's table runs itself. The adults get to have a conversation.

## Tip 10: Light Beeswax Candles as the Evening Begins

Easter Sunday in Queensland and northern NSW is typically warm enough to stay outdoors through the evening. As the light fades to the beautiful autumn amber of April on the Gold Coast, beeswax candles — in terracotta tones, or simple ivory — lit on the outdoor table create an Easter evening atmosphere that is genuinely its own thing. Not Christmas. Not summer. A warm autumn evening with the candles and the flowers and the last of the chocolate.

Beeswax candles from a local market or online: $12–$25 for a set of three. More beautiful than paraffin. Slower burning. A better scent.

## Tip 11: End With a Roast Lamb and Pavlova

The Easter Sunday outdoor lunch that is most distinctly Australian: roasted lamb as the main course (the traditional Easter meat in most Australian families), served with salads rather than heavy winter sides, followed by pavlova with cream and fresh autumn berries. The meal is simultaneously traditional (lamb for Easter, pavlova as a dessert occasion) and distinctly Australian in its outdoor, salad-and-garden context.

## Tip 12: Keep the Decorating Simple — Easter Is About the Gathering

The best Easter gatherings I've hosted have been the simplest: a beautiful table, fresh flowers, good food, and the full four-day weekend to enjoy it. Easter outdoor decorating serves the gathering — it doesn't replace it. A few considered elements (the hessian runner, the dahlia centrepiece, the terracotta egg holders, the beeswax candles) are all that's needed.
