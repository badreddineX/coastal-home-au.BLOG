Most Australian backyards are quietly wasted. We mow the lawn, maybe drag out a plastic chair, and leave it at that. But the [Australian Native Plant Society (ANPSA)](https://anpsa.org.au/) estimates over 25,000 species of native plants are suited to Australian garden conditions — most require a fraction of the water, maintenance, and fertiliser of exotic species. When I landscaped my first backyard in Queensland, I had no idea where to start. What I learned is that a few clear decisions, done in the right order, changes everything. This guide walks through exactly that: zone planning, plants, paving, lighting, water features, and which bits are genuinely worth hiring out.

## 1. Define Your Zones Before You Spend Anything

This is the step most people skip, and it's why so many Australian backyards feel cluttered and purposeless. Draw a rough sketch of your space and allocate four zones: an entertaining area for dining and lounging, garden beds for planting, a lawn or turf zone if you want greenery underfoot, and a utility corner for bins, the clothesline, and the shed.

Getting these zones clear on paper costs nothing. Every decision you make after this, from paving to plant selection, becomes simpler and more deliberate. I spent two hours with a pencil and a tape measure before I touched a single shovel, and it saved me weeks of second-guessing.

**For renters:** Focus on container gardens and portable raised beds. These are fully removable and can transform an outdoor space without touching the ground. A few large pots with natives and a folding timber table can create a genuinely beautiful zone with zero permanent changes.

## 2. Which Native Plants Work Best for Australian Coastal Gardens?

The [Australian Native Plant Society (ANPSA)](https://anpsa.org.au/) documents over 25,000 native species suited to Australian conditions. Many coastal varieties establish in under one season and survive on rainfall alone once their root systems are set, making them far more economical than imported exotic species over a three-to-five year horizon. ([ANPSA](https://anpsa.org.au/), 2025)

In my Queensland garden, I planted Lomandra along the fence line in April and it was thriving without irrigation by November. It's the most forgiving plant I've ever grown.

Start with these proven performers:

- **Lomandra** — tough, grass-like, perfect as a border or mass planting along fences
- **Westringia (Coastal Rosemary)** — ideal for low hedging, salt-tolerant, flowers most of the year
- **Grevillea** — flowering, attracts honeyeaters, dozens of sizes and forms available
- **Coastal Banksia** — structural, dramatic, and genuinely stunning in a coastal garden

For groundcover between beds, try Pigface (Carpobrotus) or coastal grasses like Poa and Themeda. [Garden Express](https://www.gardenexpress.com.au/) ships natives Australia-wide, which is handy if your local Bunnings has a limited selection.

**Bold key point:** A well-planted native garden bed along a 10m fence line typically costs ~$150–$400 AUD in plants and mulch, and requires almost no maintenance after the first season.

## 3. Lawn vs Artificial Turf: Which Suits Your Backyard?

Real lawn performs well in full sun with decent rainfall or irrigation access. It's cooler underfoot in summer, better for kids and barefoot living, and costs less upfront. But in shady backyards, high-traffic areas, or properties on water restrictions, real grass becomes a losing battle. I've replaced patchy real lawn with artificial turf twice, and both times it was the right call.

Artificial turf now looks far better than it did five years ago. The best products have a natural tone variation and a realistic blade length that passes a casual glance. Quality turf runs ~$30–$55 AUD per square metre installed, and lasts 10–15 years with minimal upkeep.

**Bold key point:** For a 20sqm area, budget ~$600–$1,100 AUD for DIY artificial turf including base preparation, or ~$1,200–$2,000 AUD installed professionally.

If you're in a hot climate zone, consider leaving a turf-free zone near the house. Hard surfaces retain heat; lawn stays cooler.

## 4. How Do You Choose the Right Paving for an Australian Backyard?

The ground surface is the single largest visual element in your backyard. It sets the tone for everything else, so it's worth getting right.

**Paving options that work well in Australian conditions:**

- **Concrete pavers** — affordable, practical, available everywhere via [Bunnings](https://www.bunnings.com.au/our-range/garden). Budget ~$30–$60 AUD per sqm.
- **Sandstone pavers** — warm honey tones that suit coastal Australian homes beautifully. ~$60–$120 AUD per sqm.
- **Timber decking** — brings warmth and suits entertaining areas. Hardwood decking runs ~$200–$350 AUD per sqm installed.
- **Compacted gravel** — the most affordable option, great for casual zones and garden pathways

A combination approach usually works best: paving in the entertaining zone, lawn or turf for the family play area, gravel or mulch for garden pathways. [Beaumont Tiles](https://www.beaumont-tiles.com.au/outdoor-tiles) has an excellent range of outdoor pavers if you want something more refined than the standard Bunnings range.

## 5. Garden Bed Edging: The Detail That Lifts the Whole Backyard

The single most underrated backyard upgrade is clean garden bed edging. It makes a $200 planting job look like a $2,000 landscaping project. Most people overlook it entirely, and it shows.

Use a garden hose to experiment with curved shapes before you commit to any digging. Curves look more natural and considered than straight lines in a residential garden. Once you're happy with the shape, install steel garden edging for a modern, low-profile finish, or sandstone boulder edging for something warmer and more coastal. Both are available at [Bunnings](https://www.bunnings.com.au/our-range/garden).

After edging, mulch heavily, at least 6–8cm deep. Mulch suppresses weeds, retains soil moisture through summer heat, and makes the whole garden look tended without ongoing effort.

**Bold key point:** Steel garden edging costs ~$15–$25 AUD per 5m length. Mulch for a standard 10sqm bed runs ~$40–$80 AUD. Together, they're one of the best-value landscaping investments you can make.

## 6. Are Vertical Gardens Worth It for Small Australian Backyards?

Vertical gardens solve the small backyard problem better than almost any other landscaping technique. They take up no floor space, add lush greenery to blank fence lines, and can double as privacy screening against neighbouring properties.

Wall-mounted planting panels are the most practical option. A basic modular system from a retailer like [Temple & Webster](https://www.templeandwebster.com.au/Outdoor-Furniture-C27170.html) runs ~$80–$250 AUD, and you can plant it with trailing natives, herbs, or succulents depending on your light conditions.

Alternatively, train a fast-growing climber, such as Hardenbergia or Native Jasmine, up a simple trellis. This is the cheapest and most natural-looking option, and both species are available through [Garden Express](https://www.gardenexpress.com.au/).

**Bold key point:** A 1.2m x 2m vertical garden panel planted with succulents or herbs costs ~$120–$300 AUD all-in, and adds substantial visual impact to a bare fence.

## 7. Water Features: Do They Work in Australian Backyards?

A water feature is one of those backyard additions that sounds indulgent but delivers real, everyday value. The sound of moving water is genuinely calming, and it masks street noise better than most fencing solutions.

The simplest and most practical option is a self-contained pot fountain. These are solar-powered, require no plumbing, and can be moved around until you find the right position. They start from ~$150 AUD at [Bunnings](https://www.bunnings.com.au/our-range/garden). I added one to a corner of my garden where the fence reflected traffic noise, and it changed the feel of the whole space.

If you want something more permanent, a simple rill or raised pond is a weekend project with great results. A wall-mounted water feature works well in smaller backyards where floor space is tight.

**Bold key point:** A quality solar pot fountain costs ~$150–$400 AUD and requires no electrician, no plumbing, and no ongoing running costs beyond occasional cleaning.

## 8. Landscape Lighting: What Makes the Biggest Difference After Dark?

According to landscape design research cited by the Australian Institute of Landscape Architects, outdoor lighting can extend usable backyard hours by an estimated 40–60% across autumn and winter months, making it one of the highest-return additions for year-round outdoor living. ([AILA](https://www.aila.org.au/), 2024)

Good lighting transforms how a backyard feels at night. It also significantly extends how much you actually use the space, especially through the cooler months.

Start with path lights along garden bed edges and stepping stone routes. Add uplights pointing into feature trees or large shrubs for drama. String lights across the entertaining area create warmth and signal "this is a place to sit and stay." If you have steps or level changes, step lights are both practical and stylish.

Solar-powered options have improved enormously in recent years. The Bunnings range now includes solar path lights and garden spots that look clean, charge reliably in Australian sun, and actually stay lit through the evening.

**Bold key point:** A complete solar lighting setup, including 6 path lights and string lights across a 4m span, costs ~$80–$200 AUD from [Bunnings](https://www.bunnings.com.au/our-range/garden) with no electrician required.

## 9. Composting and Sustainability: Small Effort, Real Impact

Australian households send an estimated 7.3 million tonnes of organic waste to landfill each year, according to the Australian Bureau of Statistics. Composting at home diverts food and garden waste from that stream while producing free, high-quality soil amendment for garden beds.

A simple compost bin costs ~$50–$120 AUD from Bunnings and takes about 10 minutes per week to manage. Feed it with kitchen scraps, grass clippings, and garden prunings. In 8–12 weeks you have a soil conditioner that improves drainage, feeds plants, and reduces the need for store-bought fertiliser.

If a full compost bin isn't practical, a worm farm works in smaller spaces and produces both castings and liquid fertiliser. Both options align your garden with the ethos of native planting: low input, high return.

**Bold key point:** Composting costs ~$50–$120 AUD to set up and saves an estimated ~$100–$200 AUD per year in fertiliser and potting mix purchases.

## 10. DIY Weekend Projects vs Hiring a Contractor: Where's the Line?

I've done both, and I've got the mistakes to prove it. The honest answer is that most planting, mulching, edging, and lighting is genuinely DIY-friendly. The work that's worth paying for is anything structural: paving, decking, retaining walls, and irrigation systems.

Here's how I'd split it for a typical Australian backyard project:

**Do yourself:** Zone planning, garden bed creation and edging, native planting, mulching, water features, lighting, composting setup, and styling.

**Hire out:** Paving and concrete work, timber decking construction, retaining walls over 600mm high, hardwired electrical, and irrigation if it involves trenching.

A landscaper for structural work typically runs ~$450–$800 AUD per day in most Australian cities. Getting quotes from two or three contractors before committing is standard practice and usually saves 10–20% on the final price.

**Bold key point:** By doing your own planting, mulching, and lighting, you can realistically save ~$2,000–$5,000 AUD on a mid-range backyard project compared to full contractor quotes.

## 11. Styling the Entertaining Zone: Furniture and Finishing Touches

The physical landscaping sets the stage, but the furniture and accessories make it feel lived-in and worth spending time in. Keep the entertaining zone simple: a table, chairs, and a shade solution are the core.

For outdoor furniture that handles Australian UV and humidity, look at [Temple & Webster](https://www.templeandwebster.com.au/Outdoor-Furniture-C27170.html) for a wide range at different price points, or [Outdoor Elegance](https://www.outdoorelegance.com.au/) for higher-end teak and aluminium pieces that genuinely last.

A large outdoor rug defines the entertaining space without adding bulk. A few statement pots, rather than many small ones, have far more visual impact. And a single large shade umbrella or a sail shade makes the difference between a zone you use at 2pm in January and one you avoid.

**Bold key point:** A complete entertaining zone setup, including a 6-seat table and chairs, umbrella, rug, and two large pots, typically runs ~$800–$2,500 AUD from mid-range retailers.

## 12. When to Call in a Landscape Designer

Most people think landscape designers are for large budgets and complex projects. In practice, a single two-hour consultation, which typically runs ~$200–$400 AUD, can save you far more than that in avoided mistakes and poor plant placement decisions.

If your backyard has significant slope, drainage problems, or an awkward shape, a designer's input is genuinely worth the cost. They'll spot issues you won't, suggest plant combinations suited to your specific microclimate, and give you a sequenced plan you can execute over multiple weekends and budgets.

The [Australian Institute of Landscape Architects](https://www.aila.org.au/) has a "Find a Member" directory to locate qualified designers in your state.

**Bold key point:** A landscape design consultation typically costs ~$200–$400 AUD for a 2-hour session, and most designers provide a basic plan you can execute yourself over time.
