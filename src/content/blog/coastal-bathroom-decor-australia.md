The bathroom is the most overlooked room for coastal styling — and the most renter-friendly room to transform. A [Choice.com.au](https://www.choice.com.au/home-improvement/renovating/bathrooms/articles/bathroom-renovation-guide) bathroom renovation survey found that Australians spend an average of $17,000 on bathroom renovations, but the most impactful coastal bathroom transformations I've seen cost under $300 using accessories alone. I transformed the bathroom in my Gold Coast rental without touching a single tile, and the result looked like a boutique coastal hotel. The same palette principles that work here apply throughout the home — the full [coastal home decor guide for Australia](/blog/06-coastal-home-decor-australia) covers every room from the living room to the kitchen. Here's exactly how to do it.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - The bathroom is the easiest room to style coastally without renovating
> - Australians average $17,000 on bathroom renos ([Choice.com.au](https://www.choice.com.au/home-improvement/renovating/bathrooms/articles/bathroom-renovation-guide)), but accessories alone can cost under $300
> - Towels, a rattan mirror, one plant, and a diffuser are the four highest-impact changes
> - Renters can do every step in this guide without losing their bond

## 1. Start With the Colour Palette

Getting the colours right costs nothing and changes everything. A coastal bathroom palette keeps white as the dominant base, layering soft blue or aqua as an accent, sandy beige in textiles and timber, and natural green from plants. The same palette logic applies to the [coastal bedroom](/blog/coastal-bedroom-decor-australia) next door — consistent tones across adjoining rooms create a cohesive coastal home. Avoid saturated teals or anything too dark — coastal bathrooms should feel like early morning sunlight, not a hotel lobby.

**Bold key point:** Pale sky blue works harder than any other accent colour in a coastal bathroom. It reads calmer in a small space than deep navy and pairs with everything from chrome fittings to rattan mirrors.

Renters: the palette lives entirely in accessories and textiles. You don't need to paint a single wall. Towels, a bath mat, and a candle in the right tones will do the job.

## 2. Which Tiles Work Best for a Coastal Bathroom?

If you're renovating (or helping an owner-occupier friend), tiles are where the coastal bathroom really earns its name. [Beaumont Tiles](https://www.beaumont-tiles.com.au/) carries terrazzo, zellige, and stone-look ranges that deliver genuine coastal texture without importing from overseas.

**For feature walls:** zellige in white or soft blue gives handmade texture variation that no flat tile can match. Subway tiles in a classic brick pattern are timeless. Terrazzo in neutral tones is increasingly popular in Australian coastal builds.

**For floors:** large-format stone-look tiles in warm beige or grey, hexagonal mosaic in white or sandy tone, or classic black-and-white hex all read as authentically coastal.

**Bold key point:** Beaumont Tiles' Zellige White range starts at around ~$95–$140 per square metre — less than many imported alternatives and available in most Australian capital cities.

Renters: skip this section and put that energy into Section 3.

## 3. What Mirror Should You Choose?

A rattan or timber-framed mirror is the single highest-impact coastal accessory you can add to a bathroom. It works over any existing mirror, suits any wall colour, and costs a fraction of a renovation. [Temple & Webster](https://www.templeandwebster.com.au/Bathroom-Accessories-C1811429.html) stocks a solid range from ~$45–$150 AUD, including round rattan frames in several sizes.

I went with a 70cm round rattan frame mirror from Temple & Webster in my Gold Coast bathroom. It went up with two Command strips, cost $89, and was the single most commented-on change every visitor noticed.

**Bold key point:** Round frames suit most bathroom proportions better than rectangular ones — they soften the hard angles of a standard builder's bathroom without fighting the existing lines.

Renters: Command strips rated for the frame weight are bond-safe and work on most painted walls. Test a corner first.

## 4. How Does Lighting Change the Feel?

Lighting shifts the entire mood of a bathroom, and it's easier to update than most people realise. Warm white globes (2700K–3000K) make coastal bathrooms feel relaxed and sun-washed. Cool daylight globes (5000K+) feel clinical and flattering, but not particularly coastal.

**Bold key point:** Swapping a standard globe for a warm Edison-style bulb costs ~$8–$20 AUD per fitting at Bunnings or Kmart, and the difference is immediately visible.

If your bathroom has a single overhead light and nothing else, a plug-in wall sconce or a rechargeable lamp on a shelf can add the layered lighting that makes a bathroom feel designed rather than functional. Kmart stocks rechargeable touch lamps from ~$15–$29 AUD.

Renters: globe swaps are always renter-friendly. Just keep the originals and swap them back on move-out day.

## 5. Which Towels Make the Biggest Difference?

In my experience testing coastal bathroom styling across several Australian homes, towels consistently deliver the highest visible return for money spent — more than any other single accessory. The difference between a standard cotton towel and a white waffle-weave hung neatly on a hook is genuinely dramatic.

[Adairs](https://www.adairs.com.au/bathroom/) waffle weave towels in white or sand tones start from ~$20–$60 AUD per towel, depending on size. They're thicker than standard hotel towels, dry faster, and look coastal whether they're folded or hanging.

**Bold key point:** Buy two bath towels, two hand towels, and one bath sheet in white or sandy linen tone. That's a complete set for ~$120–$180 AUD at Adairs and it transforms a bathroom shelf or towel rail instantly.

Skip beachy printed towels with shells or anchors. Solid waffle weave looks more refined and more genuinely coastal.

## 6. What Plants Thrive in a Bathroom?

Bathrooms are brilliant for plants. The humidity that makes you run the exhaust fan suits many tropical varieties perfectly. You don't need a window — several great options handle low light without complaint.

- **Pothos** — almost impossible to kill, trails beautifully from a shelf or hanging planter (~$10–$18 AUD at Bunnings)
- **Peace lily** — loves low light and humid conditions, also filters air quality (~$12–$25 AUD)
- **Boston fern** — thrives in humidity, looks wonderfully lush on a timber bath shelf
- **ZZ plant** — handles low light and irregular watering, ideal for windowless bathrooms

**Bold key point:** One plant in a simple terracotta or ceramic pot costs ~$10–$25 AUD total and adds more life to a bathroom than almost any purchased accessory.

![Bathroom shelf styled with rattan basket, white ceramic soap dish, and a pothos plant](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1507652313519-d4e9174996dd?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&q=80)

Renters: plants are completely bond-safe. Just use a waterproof saucer under the pot to protect any timber shelving.

## 7. How Should You Handle Accessories and Storage?

Replacing plastic containers and chrome accessories with natural materials is one of the cheapest ways to shift the feel of a bathroom. The goal is to remove anything that reads as synthetic or purely functional.

Swap out: plastic soap dispensers, wire caddies, chrome toothbrush holders.

Bring in: ceramic or stone soap dishes (~$15–$35 AUD), woven rattan baskets for storage (~$12–$45 AUD from Kmart or Temple & Webster), timber or bamboo trays for the vanity top.

**Bold key point:** Kmart's rattan and woven storage baskets start from ~$12–$25 AUD and are genuinely good quality for the price. A set of three in different sizes on open shelving looks styled and purposeful.

Renters: all of this is portable. Take it with you when you move.

## 8. Does Scent Really Matter?

Yes — more than most people expect. A coastal bathroom isn't just visual. Scent completes the sensory picture and makes the room feel intentionally designed rather than accidentally pleasant.

The best coastal scents for Australian bathrooms: sea salt and driftwood (the most authentic), coastal pine (fresh and distinctly Australian), and white tea or jasmine (lighter, suits smaller spaces).

[Ecoya](https://www.ecoya.com.au/) is the Australian brand I return to most often. Their Lotus Flower and Cotton & Clove reed diffusers work particularly well in bathrooms. Glasshouse and Circa Home are excellent alternatives, all available at Myer and David Jones.

**Bold key point:** A quality reed diffuser from Ecoya costs ~$35–$65 AUD and lasts three to four months. That's coastal scent for less than $20 per month — more effective and longer-lasting than a candle for a daily-use bathroom.

## 9. What About Fixtures and Fittings?

If you're renovating or replacing worn fittings, go for brushed nickel or matte black over chrome. Both read as more considered and hold up better visually against natural textures like rattan and timber. Chrome isn't a deal-breaker — the right accessories around it will soften any clinical edge.

**Bold key point:** A freestanding bath in white or stone composite is the ultimate coastal statement if your space and budget allow. Round baths are having a genuine moment in Australian coastal interior design right now.

Renters: fixtures are out of scope. Focus your energy on the accessory layers in the other sections — they'll do more work than you'd expect.

---

## 10. Bath Mats: The Finishing Detail

A timber or stone-look bath mat is one of the last things most people think about and one of the easiest upgrades to make. Standard cotton bath mats read as generic. A teak timber mat or a textured stone-look option reads as deliberately designed.

Teak mats are naturally mould-resistant, dry faster than cotton, and look good for years. Temple & Webster and Adairs both stock them in the ~$29–$89 AUD range.

**Bold key point:** Teak bath mats need minimal care — a light sand every few months keeps them looking fresh. They're also a useful indicator of bathroom ventilation: if yours is going dark, you need more airflow after showers.

## Start Here: Priority Order

| Priority | Action | Approx. Cost |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| 1 | Replace towels with white or sandy waffle weave | ~$20–$60 AUD |
| 2 | Add a rattan or timber-framed mirror | ~$45–$150 AUD |
| 3 | Place one humidity-loving plant (pothos or peace lily) | ~$10–$25 AUD |
| 4 | Add a coastal scent diffuser | ~$35–$65 AUD |
| 5 | Upgrade to a timber or stone-look bath mat | ~$29–$89 AUD |

The order above matters. Most styling guides suggest mirrors or plants first. In my experience, towels win — they cover more surface area, change the colour palette fastest, and guests notice them before anything else.
