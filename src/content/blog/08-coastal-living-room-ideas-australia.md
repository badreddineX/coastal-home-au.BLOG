When we renovated our Gold Coast living room three years ago, I had a clear brief: it needed to look like the Australian coast felt — light, relaxed, genuinely beautiful without trying hard. The previous look was dark timber, leather sofa, and a colour palette that belonged in a Sydney office, not a coastal Queensland home.

Here's what I changed, what I kept, and what I spent.

![Coastal Australian living room with natural linen sofa, large jute rug, rattan armchair, and linen curtains](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1505691938895-1758d7feb511?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&q=80)

## Tip 1: Start With the Sofa — It Defines Everything Else

The sofa is the largest piece in any living room, which means it has the greatest influence on the space. In a coastal living room, the sofa should be: large, linen-upholstered or linen-covered, and in white, natural, or warm grey. Everything else — the rug, the cushions, the art — is chosen in relation to the sofa.

I replaced our dark leather sofa with a 3.5-seater linen sofa from Temple & Webster in natural/oatmeal tone ($1,899). The transformation was immediate and dramatic — the room gained 30% of its apparent size and the light changed character entirely.

**Budget option:** Kmart 3-seater sofa in light grey or beige fabric ($399–$599). The quality isn't the same but the visual contribution at entry-level is genuine. Upgrade the cushion covers and it reads well.

## Tip 2: Lay the Largest Jute Rug You Can Fit

The jute rug is to the coastal living room what the sofa is to the furniture arrangement — the anchor that everything else relates to. In our living room (5.2×4.1m), I have a 240×340cm jute rug from Temple & Webster ($329). It sits under the sofa's front legs, under the full footprint of the coffee table, and extends beyond the armchairs on each side.

The size surprised me when I first rolled it out. Then I realised it was exactly right — the larger scale pulls the furniture together and makes the room feel cohesive rather than assembled.

**The rule:** In a 4×4m+ living room, go 240×340cm or larger. In a smaller room (3×3.5m), 200×290cm minimum. Almost every coastal living room mistake I've seen involves a rug that's too small.

## Tip 3: Hang Linen Curtains at Ceiling Height

This single change — moving the curtain rod from above the window to the ceiling — is responsible for the most dramatic visual improvement in any of the rooms I've changed. I installed ceiling-mounted brackets (Bunnings, $8–$15 each) and hung IKEA AINA natural linen curtains (two pairs per window, for fullness) floor to ceiling.

The living room now feels closer to 3m in height than the 2.4m it actually has. The light through linen in the morning is extraordinary — soft, warm, and diffuse.

**Cost:** IKEA AINA linen curtain pair (145×250cm): $99 per pair. Two pairs per window (four panels total): $198 per window. Ceiling brackets from Bunnings: $8–$15 each.

## Tip 4: Add a Rattan Armchair as a Second Seating Option

The rattan armchair is the most visually recognisable coastal living room element. I added one — a natural rattan and linen armchair from Temple & Webster ($395) — beside the sofa in the corner nearest the window. It creates a reading nook quality within the broader living room and adds the material texture that linen alone doesn't provide.

**The placement rule:** The armchair should be at a slight angle to the sofa, not parallel to it. The angled position creates a natural conversational zone and makes the corner feel intentional rather than space-filling.

## Tip 5: Style the Coffee Table in Layers of Three

The coffee table in a coastal living room is styled in the same way as a bedside table or a shelf — in layers, odd numbers, and relating to the material palette. My current coffee table styling: a stack of coastal interior books + a small terracotta pot with a trailing succulent + a woven rattan coaster set. Three elements, different heights, from the same natural material family.

What not to do: a single item (underwhelming), too many items (cluttered), or items that don't relate to the coastal palette (random decorative objects that break the cohesion).

## Tip 6: Introduce a Rattan or Bamboo Pendant Light

Overhead lighting in most Australian living rooms is a single ceiling fixture that does nothing for atmosphere. A rattan pendant light hung lower over the coffee table/lounge zone changes everything — it creates a pool of warm light at sitting height, draws the eye up while also anchoring the seating zone, and adds material texture to a room that otherwise only has it at floor and surface level.

**What I use:** A large rattan pendant (55cm diameter) from Temple & Webster ($149), hung on a cord extension to bring it from ceiling height (2.4m) to approximately 1.8m above the coffee table. The effect is a warm, intimate pool of light that makes the living room feel like an evening destination.

## Tip 7: Choose Cushions in Texture Variation, Not Colour Variation

The coastal living room cushion approach that creates the most cohesive result: all cushions in the same colour family (white, natural, and one accent tone such as sage or soft blue), but in different textures (smooth linen, waffle weave, chunky knit, open weave).

I have seven cushions on our sofa: three in white smooth linen, two in natural waffle-weave linen, and two in sage green open-weave linen. All from Adairs, total cost approximately $180. The variation in texture creates visual richness; the consistency in colour creates cohesion.

## Tip 8: Add a Large Indoor Plant in the Corner

A large fiddle leaf fig (2m+) in the corner of our living room is the most frequently commented-on element of the room. Plants in a coastal living room do the same work as rattan and jute — they connect the interior to the natural landscape outside. A single large plant in a statement position achieves more than a collection of small plants scattered across surfaces.

**What to buy:** A mature fiddle leaf fig from a nursery costs $60–$180 depending on size. A Zanzibar gem (ZZ plant) is a more forgiving option at $25–$60 from Bunnings. Both need a simple terracotta or ceramic pot — not plastic, and not a pot that competes visually with the plant.

## Tip 9: Edit the Decorative Objects to One Third

The coastal living room edit is as important as the additions. I went through every surface — coffee table, shelves, sideboards — and reduced the number of objects by 60%. The 40% that remained were the natural-material pieces: ceramics, woven baskets, a few books with interesting covers, a single candle. Everything synthetic or visually disconnected from the palette went into a box.

The room looked immediately better. More edited rooms always do.

## Tip 10: Use a Sideboard or Media Unit in White or Pale Timber

The TV unit or sideboard in a coastal living room should disappear rather than compete. White painted timber or pale oak are the two options that work. Dark timber units anchor the room with weight rather than lightness and contradict the airy coastal aesthetic.

I painted our existing dark timber media unit in Dulux Vivid White (chalk paint, no sanding required) in an afternoon. Total cost: $45 for a litre of chalk paint from a local art supplier. The transformation took the unit from the most visually heavy element in the room to something that recedes into the background where it belongs.

## Tip 11: Let Natural Light Be the Primary Light Source

The best-lit coastal living rooms I've been in use electric light as a supplement to natural light, not a replacement for it. This means: prioritise window coverage (linen sheers rather than block-out curtains during the day), position reflective surfaces (mirrors, glass, light ceramics) to bounce light around the room, and only switch on electric lights at the point where natural light becomes insufficient.

I added a large arched mirror (80×180cm, $279 from Temple & Webster) opposite our main window. The room gained what felt like a second window — and the light quality through linen reflected off a white-painted wall is genuinely beautiful.

## Tip 12: Choose a Coastal Scent for the Room

The sensory detail that most reinforces a coastal aesthetic without any visual change: diffusing a coastal scent in the living room. I use a reed diffuser in "Driftwood & Sea Salt" from Reed St Collective ($49, Temple & Webster). The scent is subtle, non-synthetic, and genuinely evocative of the coast. It's the first thing guests notice when they walk in and — regularly — the first thing they ask about.

## Tip 13: Make the Outdoor Connection Visible

The finest coastal living rooms in Australia have direct visual and physical access to the outdoor space. A sliding glass door to the alfresco, open during the day, is the best possible backdrop to a coastal interior — the living room literally extends to the garden or alfresco, and the indoor-outdoor continuity is what the Australian coastal lifestyle is fundamentally about.

If the connection isn't architectural, approximate it: position the sofa to face the garden view rather than the TV, use plants and materials that echo the outside, and if possible, keep outdoor furniture in the same colour palette as the indoor furniture so the transition feels seamless.
